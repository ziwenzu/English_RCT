	\begin{abstract}
		\noindent Classic accounts of authoritarian censorship imply that access to censored foreign media should weaken regime support. I test this claim with a 12-week field experiment embedded in a Chinese English test-preparation app, randomizing 6,365 users to receive, at varying doses, pro-China, anti-China, apolitical China-related, or neutral non-China content from foreign outlets censored by the Chinese government. By holding source type constant and varying message valence, the design identifies when censored foreign media undermine or reinforce authoritarian resilience. Pro-China coverage increases regime legitimacy, trust in censored foreign media, and willingness to consume more such content, with effects that persist after the intervention ends. Anti-China coverage lowers regime support on average, but its effects are concentrated among ideologically receptive respondents, offset by identity backlash, and largely dissipate by follow-up. Under authoritarianism, the political effects of censored foreign media depend on narrative valence, prior predispositions, and acceptance rather than source identity alone.
	\end{abstract}
	
	
	
	\textbf{Keywords}: Authoritarianism; Censorship; China; Media; Public Opinion

	\section*{Introduction}
	
Information control is central to authoritarian rule. Regimes censor opposition, privilege regime-compatible narratives, and shape what citizens learn about domestic politics and the outside world \citep{BesleyPrat2006,gurievtreisman2019,huang2015signal,kingpanroberts2013,roberts2018,Roberts2020ARPS,stockmann2013}. Yet censorship rarely produces full informational closure.\footnote{Citizens may still encounter such content through virtual private networks (VPNs), reposted screenshots, translated excerpts, and transnational educational or professional networks \citep{chenyang2019,DalNisbet2022SMS,hobbsroberts2018,PanSiegel2020APSR,Roberts2020ARPS}.} The core question is conditional: when does exposure to freer foreign media weaken regime support, when is it discounted, and when does it instead reinforce authoritarian resilience?

Existing research yields competing expectations. One view holds that freer foreign media should weaken regime support by revealing information the regime seeks to suppress and allowing citizens to update away from official narratives \citep{BesleyPrat2006,EgorovGurievSonin2009,gurievtreisman2019,chenyang2019}. Comparative evidence from East Germany and Russia also shows that foreign media can shift political attitudes under authoritarian rule, though not always in the same direction \citep{KernHainmueller2009,EnikolopovPetrovaZhuravskaya2011}. But outside information is not uniformly liberalizing. It can reduce trust in the regime, yet it can also improve domestic evaluations by shifting comparative benchmarks or correcting inflated beliefs about conditions abroad \citep{bursztynyang2022,DuchStevenson2008,hobbsroberts2018,huang2015international,huangyeh2017,KayserPeress2012}. Classic work on preference falsification further implies that publicly expressed support can remain stable until new signals make private dissent safer to reveal \citep{Kuran1991,Kuran1995}. More generally, citizens are selective rather than passive recipients of political information.\footnote{This point draws on work on selective exposure, source credibility, motivated reasoning, and preference falsification dynamics \citep{BakshyMessingAdamic2015,Druckman2001,GentzkowShapiro2006,Kuran1995,Levendusky2013,LupiaMcCubbins1998CUP,Prior2007CUP,taberlodge2006,wangwestwood2024,zaller1992}.} I argue that the political effects of freer foreign media depend on message valence, prior predispositions, and audience acceptance.

China is a particularly consequential setting for this question. It combines one of the world’s most restrictive information environments with continued, if uneven, access to censored foreign content through VPNs and related tools \citep{ChangEtAl2022,FreedomHouse2024,hobbsroberts2018}. Because the Great Firewall restricts major foreign news sites, even limited exposure to freer foreign media may have meaningful political consequences in a polity of more than 1.4 billion people. I study these dynamics in a 12-week field experiment embedded in a large Chinese English test-preparation app.\footnote{The experimental design and main hypotheses were preregistered at \url{https://osf.io/yab8j}.} The setting is useful because users preparing for the Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL), International English Language Testing System (IELTS), Graduate Record Examinations (GRE), Graduate Management Admission Test (GMAT), and related exams regularly consume English-language materials for study abroad, graduate admissions, and professional advancement while remaining inside a censored information environment. Rather than presenting raw news reports, the intervention adapts material from censored foreign outlets into reading-comprehension and English-training exercises.\footnote{The finalized materials bank includes 40 readings from \textit{The Guardian}, 21 from \textit{Reuters}, 12 from \textit{The Economist}, 11 from \textit{BBC Travel}, and 5 from \textit{The New York Times}.} This format captures exposure to freer foreign media in a way that is both naturalistic for participants and politically meaningful. The study recruited 12,215 users into a baseline survey and randomized 6,365 into six groups: pro-China political content at low or high dose, anti-China political content at low or high dose, apolitical China-related content, or neutral non-China content. Participants received two readings per week for twelve weeks, each paired with a short video, quiz questions, and weekly evaluations of interest, credibility, and demand for similar content. Endline surveys were completed by 5,215 participants, and a four-week follow-up survey by 2,115.

The design makes three advances. First, it separates source from message by holding outlet type constant while varying message valence. This identifies whether responses are driven by censored foreign-media exposure per se or by the narratives carried by those outlets. Second, it turns dose and durability into design features by randomizing cumulative exposure and tracking respondents over time. Third, it links attitudinal change to high-frequency behavioral evidence. Because the platform records article openings, reading time, quiz completion, weekly evaluations, and willingness to pay (WTP) for additional content, the study can separate exposure from engagement, engagement from acceptance, and persuasion from avoidance. The design identifies not only whether censored foreign media matter, but also which narratives persuade, which merely attract attention, and which sustain demand under repetition.
	
	The results show that freer foreign media are not uniformly liberalizing. At endline, pooled pro-China treatment raises the legitimacy index by \(0.075\) standard deviations (SD) and the foreign-media receptivity-and-demand index by \(0.223\) SD; pooled anti-China treatment lowers legitimacy by \(0.033\) SD and raises backlash by \(0.062\) SD. Both valences increase attention (about 14 additional reading minutes relative to control), but they diverge on acceptance and demand. Pro-China exposure increases weekly credibility ratings by \(2.900\) points and ``want more similar'' ratings by \(0.583\) points, raises WTP by about 5 RMB, and increases purchase by 11.7 percentage points. Anti-China exposure does not increase credibility or content demand, lowers WTP by 2.8 RMB, and lowers purchase by 5.1 percentage points. Persistence is similarly asymmetric: pro-China effects on regime support attenuate from \(0.270\) at endline to \(0.110\) at follow-up, whereas anti-China effects move from \(-0.203\) at endline to approximately zero at follow-up.
	
These findings make three contributions. First, they refine access-based theories of authoritarian information control \citep{BesleyPrat2006,gurievtreisman2019,roberts2018,RosenfeldWallace2024}. In this study, the decisive margin is interpretation after exposure, not access alone: anti-China content can reduce legitimacy among receptive respondents but also trigger backlash and lower future demand, whereas pro-China content from censored foreign outlets raises both legitimacy and source trust. The same mechanism helps explain why authoritarian regimes may tolerate or recirculate selected foreign content when it can be converted into domestic legitimation, and why comparable effects can differ across settings such as East Germany, Russia, and contemporary China \citep{KernHainmueller2009,EnikolopovPetrovaZhuravskaya2011,QinStrombergWu2017,kingpanroberts2013}.

Second, the paper sharpens persuasion research by separating attention, acceptance, and downstream choice \citep{ArceneauxJohnson2013,Prior2007CUP,zaller1992,taberlodge2006,ValloneRossLepper1985,Levendusky2013}. Respondents in both valence conditions consume more political content, but only accepted exposure produces credibility gains, willingness to pay, and persistent demand. This distinction matters because analyses based on clicks or viewing time alone would misclassify anti-China exposure as persuasive when much of it is attended but discounted. The findings therefore connect selective-exposure and motivated-reasoning theories to costly behavioral outcomes rather than to survey attitudes alone \citep{BakshyMessingAdamic2015,FlaxmanGoelRao2016,GonzalezBailonEtAl2023,KangSheen2025,shen2025forced}.

Third, the paper offers a unified account of outside information in China by linking censorship and propaganda in a single framework. The argument is sequential: censorship and platform governance shape which outside signals are encountered; propaganda and source cueing shape how those signals are interpreted; and acceptance dynamics determine whether effects persist or reverse into backlash. Within this sequence, foreign criticism can update beliefs against the regime, but it can also validate official narratives of external hostility; favorable foreign coverage can have larger and more persistent effects because it is both counter-stereotypic and harder to dismiss as hostile. This logic helps reconcile conflicting findings in prior China research and clarifies why selective openness to foreign information can complement, rather than contradict, domestic propaganda strategy \citep{chenyang2019,huang2015international,huangyeh2017,wangwestwood2024,QinStrombergWu2017,KingPanRoberts2017,GueorguievMalesky2019,huang2015signal,Huang2018}. The next section formalizes this acceptance-centered logic.
	
	
	\section*{Theory}
	
Censored or banned foreign media create a dual inference problem: citizens update about the regime, and about the source. In authoritarian settings, censored foreign outlets can appear informative precisely because they are censored. The same foreignness, however, makes them easy targets for official narratives of external hostility, ideological infiltration, and anti-national bias. I make three claims: attention is not acceptance, valence shifts source inferences, and persistence depends on repeated accepted exposure rather than repeated exposure alone. Political effects turn not on exposure by itself, but on how recipients map message valence onto source credibility and how those inferences interact with prior predispositions.

This claim follows from, but is not reducible to, four adjacent literatures. Models of censorship and media freedom emphasize that freer media relax authoritarian information monopolies by making suppressed facts available \citep{BesleyPrat2006,EgorovGurievSonin2009,gurievtreisman2019,chenyang2019}. Comparative work on East Germany and Russia further shows that foreign media can either erode or stabilize authoritarian rule depending on how audiences process those signals \citep{KernHainmueller2009,EnikolopovPetrovaZhuravskaya2011}. Research on international benchmarks shows that outside information can also improve evaluations of the home regime when it revises beliefs about conditions abroad \citep{KayserPeress2012,huang2015international}. Work on persuasion, selective exposure, and authoritarian propaganda shows that citizens condition on source credibility, resist dissonant information, and respond to the political meaning of the messenger as much as to factual content \citep{zaller1992,LupiaMcCubbins1998CUP,GentzkowShapiro2006,taberlodge2006,huang2015signal,mattinglyyao2022,panshaoxu2021,ValloneRossLepper1985,wangwestwood2024}. Classic preference-falsification theory adds that expressed political support can diverge from private beliefs and shift discontinuously when informational or social thresholds are crossed \citep{Kuran1991,Kuran1995}. Taken together, these literatures imply that censored foreign media are neither uniformly liberalizing nor politically inert: effects depend on whether recipients treat a message as information about the focal polity or as evidence that the source is biased against it.

The key asymmetry concerns valence. Unfavorable coverage from a censored foreign outlet is politically damaging if accepted, but inferentially ambiguous. It may reflect independent reporting of unwelcome facts, but it is also consistent with hostility toward the focal polity. Favorable coverage carries the opposite signal. It is less directly threatening to the regime, but more diagnostic of source independence because it violates the prior that foreign media are uniformly critical of the target state \citep{hovlandweiss1951,GentzkowShapiro2006}. Favorable coverage can therefore be harder to dismiss even when its informational content is less threatening. Valence matters not only because messages differ substantively, but because they differ in what they reveal about the speaker.

From this perspective, the relevant margin is \emph{acceptance}, not exposure. Citizens may read politically charged foreign content because it is novel, provocative, or identity-relevant without treating it as credible. Political updating depends on whether engagement converts into acceptance. Persistence depends on whether accepted exposure also raises willingness to return to the source. This distinction matters because the design separately observes article opening, time spent, weekly credibility evaluations, and later demand. The theoretical task is to explain when censored foreign content attracts attention, when it is internalized, and when repetition produces persuasion rather than backlash or avoidance. Next, I build a minimal model of this mechanism and map it directly onto the experiment.
	
	\subsection*{A Minimal Model of Attention, Acceptance, and Demand}
	
The model is intentionally minimal and recipient-side.\footnote{A richer model could endogenize media production and censorship. For the present paper, the recipient-side problem is the relevant object because the design holds source type constant while randomizing message valence and dose.} It formalizes the inferential problem created by censored foreign media rather than offering a full theory of media production. In each period, recipient \(i\) is assigned one item. Before deciding whether to open it, the recipient observes a preview \(h_t=(c_t,\tilde v_t)\), where \(c_t\in\{0,1\}\) indicates whether the item concerns the focal polity and \(\tilde v_t\in\{-1,0,+1\}\) is a headline-level cue about whether the item appears unfavorable to it, non-directional, or favorable to it.\footnote{The preview/full-message distinction is included because recipients often decide whether to open political content on the basis of titles, summaries, or other cues before seeing the full text. The valence index records the direction of the content toward the focal polity rather than its objective truth value.} After opening, the recipient observes the full valence \(v_t\in\{-1,0,+1\}\). The neutral benchmark corresponds to \(c_t=0\) and \(v_t=0\); non-directional focal-polity content corresponds to \(c_t=1\) and \(v_t=0\).
	
		Let source type be \(s\in\{I,H\}\), where \(I\) denotes an independent outlet and \(H\) a hostile one.\footnote{This latent type is recipient-perceived rather than an objective coding of outlet quality. The same publisher can be treated as more independent by one respondent and more hostile by another, depending on prior beliefs and cueing.} Recipients do not observe \(s\), but begin period \(t\) with prior \(\mu_{it}=\Pr(s=I)\). For focal-polity content, let \(\ell_j(v)=\Pr(v_t=v\mid s=j,c_t=1)\) denote the likelihood of observing valence \(v\) under source type \(j\). The only restriction needed is a partial-pooling condition:
	\[
	\frac{\ell_I(+1)}{\ell_H(+1)}>\frac{\ell_I(-1)}{\ell_H(-1)}.
	\]
		An independent outlet may publish either favorable or unfavorable news depending on the facts, whereas a hostile outlet is relatively more likely to publish unfavorable content. After observing valence \(v_t\), the recipient's posterior that the source is independent is
	\[
	q_{it}(v_t)=\Pr(s=I\mid v_t,c_t=1)=\frac{\mu_{it}\ell_I(v_t)}{\mu_{it}\ell_I(v_t)+(1-\mu_{it})\ell_H(v_t)}.
	\]
		The restriction above implies \(q_{it}(+1)>q_{it}(-1)\). A favorable report from a censored foreign outlet is therefore more diagnostic of source independence than an unfavorable report, even though both come from the same class of censored outside sources. This is the model's central asymmetry.
	
	Recipient \(i\) opens the item when expected utility from doing so is nonnegative:
	\[
	r_{it}=\mathbf{1}\left\{\alpha c_t+\sigma \mathbf{1}\{\tilde v_t\neq 0\}+\xi \mathbb{E}[q_{it}(v_t)\mid h_t]-c_i^R\ge 0\right\}.
	\]
		The first term captures topic salience: focal-polity items are more politically relevant than neutral content. The second captures the additional attention value of directional political content. The third captures the value of potentially informative foreign material, increasing in expected source credibility. \(c_i^R\) is the private cost of opening and processing the assignment. This immediately distinguishes focal-polity but non-directional content from the neutral benchmark, while also implying that strongly valenced political items of either sign should attract more attention than neutral ones.
	
	Conditional on exposure, acceptance depends on source credibility and on the costs of internalizing a message. Let \(p_i\in[0,1]\) index nationalism or prior regime support, with larger values indicating more nationalist and regime-supportive respondents. Let \(n_{it}\ge 0\) denote susceptibility to foreign-threat interpretations. The utility from accepting the message as informative is
	\[
	U_{it}^{A}=\lambda(v_t)q_{it}(v_t)-\rho^{-}p_i\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\}-\rho^{+}(1-p_i)\mathbf{1}\{v_t=+1\}-\tau n_{it}\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\},
	\]
		where \(\lambda(\pm 1)>\lambda(0)\ge 0\) and all parameters are positive. The first term is the informational value of the message, increasing in posterior source credibility. The second and third terms capture dissonance: unfavorable messages are harder for strong nationalists to accept, while favorable messages are less congenial to respondents who begin more skeptical of the regime. The final term captures the additional penalty attached to unfavorable coverage when it is interpreted as evidence of foreign hostility rather than information. Recipient \(i\) accepts the message when \(a_{it}=\mathbf{1}\{U_{it}^{A}\ge 0\}\).
	
		Political and behavioral outcomes then evolve along distinct but related margins. Let \(L_{it}\) denote regime legitimacy. Accepted favorable messages raise legitimacy, while accepted unfavorable messages lower it:
	\[
	L_{i,t+1}=L_{it}+\beta_{+}a_{it}\mathbf{1}\{v_t=+1\}-\beta_{-}a_{it}\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\},
	\]
		with \(\beta_{+},\beta_{-}>0\). Rejected unfavorable exposure, however, need not leave attitudes unchanged. When recipients attend to hostile-seeming criticism and reject it, they can become more disposed to interpret future foreign content as threatening:
	\[
	n_{i,t+1}=n_{it}+\eta r_{it}(1-a_{it})\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\}, \qquad \eta>0.
	\]
	Observed backlash outcomes such as heightened nationalism or greater support for censorship are empirical manifestations of this rising foreign-threat disposition.\footnote{Empirically, I treat nationalism and censorship-support outcomes as observable manifestations of this latent foreign-threat channel rather than as one-to-one direct measures of \(n_{it}\).} Credibility also evolves dynamically. Let
	\[
	\mu_{i,t+1}=(1-\omega)\mu_{it}+\omega q_{it}(v_t), \qquad \omega\in(0,1],
	\]
	so that accepted counter-stereotypic signals can increase future trust in the source rather than merely shift contemporaneous attitudes.
	
	Finally, let \(D_{it}\) denote downstream demand for similar foreign content. Demand rises when exposure is accepted and when it improves the perceived credibility of the source, but falls when respondents attend to the content and reject it:
	\[
	D_{i,t+1}=D_{it}+\phi a_{it}+\psi\big(\mu_{i,t+1}-\mu_{it}\big)-\kappa r_{it}(1-a_{it}), \qquad \phi,\psi,\kappa>0.
	\]
	A later choice to seek similar content can be represented as \(d_{it}=\mathbf{1}\{D_{it}\ge \bar D_i\}\). This makes downstream demand a behavioral consequence of persuasion rather than a mere taste measure.\footnote{In the experiment, this demand margin maps to willingness to pay (WTP), purchase in the Becker--DeGroot--Marschak task, and later digest sign-up. The model treats these as behavioral manifestations of a common latent demand state \(D_{it}\).} A message that is read but discounted may still be memorable or provocative, but it should not produce the same willingness to pay for, opt into, or otherwise seek out more from the same kind of source.
	
The model yields four comparative statics that organize the empirical analysis. First, politically valenced focal-polity content of either sign should increase attention relative to neutral content, because it is more salient and potentially more informative. Second, favorable content from censored foreign outlets should be more likely to clear the acceptance threshold than unfavorable content, because it is more diagnostic of source independence. Third, unfavorable messages should reduce legitimacy chiefly where prior nationalism is lower, prior foreign-media credibility is higher, or foreign-threat interpretations are weaker; where those conditions do not hold, the same messages should more often be rejected and converted into backlash. Fourth, repetition should operate through cumulative accepted signals rather than cumulative assignments alone. Repeated exposure amplifies persuasion when it increases \(\sum_t a_{it}v_t\), but amplifies backlash and avoidance when it increases \(\sum_t r_{it}(1-a_{it})\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\}\). The model does not imply that unfavorable coverage has no average effect. It implies that these effects should be weaker and less durable than a pure information-revelation account predicts, because a nontrivial share of recipients process the same content as hostile rather than informative. I now apply this framework to the Chinese context.

\subsection*{Implications for China and Hypotheses}

China is a hard test for the model because its information environment amplifies both sides of the inference problem. Censorship and bans mark many foreign outlets as outside the domestic propaganda apparatus, which can increase perceived independence. At the same time, persistent state cueing frames foreign criticism as hostile and politically motivated, raising the cost of accepting dissonant information \citep{stockmann2013,weiss2014powerful,GallagherMiller2021,Wu2026}. The model therefore predicts not one average effect but a sequence: salience raises attention, source cueing shapes acceptance, and acceptance determines persistence.

This sequence is also where fear, risk, and avoidance enter. In model terms, these considerations load into the exposure-cost margin \(c_i^R\) and acceptance costs for dissonant signals. Sensitive content can thus be opened but discounted, or consumed initially and avoided later when perceived surveillance and social risks rise \citep{QinStrombergWu2017,roberts2018,wangwestwood2024}. \footnote{The key institutional condition is coexistence: partial access to outside reporting through circumvention and recirculation alongside durable state cueing about foreign-media threat. The argument does not require full informational closure; it requires that source cues remain politically salient when citizens encounter foreign content \citep{ChangEtAl2022,hobbsroberts2018,kingpanroberts2013,roberts2018,stockmann2013}.}

The first implication concerns attention. Because directional content is more salient and identity-relevant than neutral benchmark content, engagement should increase even before acceptance is determined.
\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Attention]
Relative to neutral non-China content, directional China-related content from censored foreign outlets increases attention (openings and reading time), with apolitical China-related content in between.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

The second implication concerns acceptance and demand. In China, anti-China content is easier to assimilate to hostile-source narratives, whereas pro-China content from censored outlets is more counter-stereotypic and can raise posterior source credibility. This asymmetry should appear in credibility judgments and in later willingness to return to similar content.
\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Acceptance and Demand]
Given attention, pro-China content generates higher credibility and downstream demand than anti-China content, which may attract attention but lower later demand when interpreted as hostile.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

If acceptance diverges by valence, political outcomes should diverge as well: accepted pro-China signals should increase legitimacy, while anti-China signals should have weaker and less durable average effects because discounting, backlash, and avoidance offset persuasion.
\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Valence and Legitimacy]
Relative to neutral non-China content, pro-China coverage increases regime legitimacy, while anti-China coverage decreases it more weakly and less durably; apolitical effects are smaller than directional effects.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

These average effects should be conditional on predispositions. Citizens in authoritarian settings are not ideologically uniform; variation in nationalism, prior regime support, and foreign-media priors shifts acceptance thresholds and therefore the sign and magnitude of treatment effects \citep{Kuran1995,PanSiegel2020APSR,wangwestwood2024}.
\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Predispositions and Backlash]
Anti-China effects are most negative among less nationalist respondents and more likely to produce backlash among strong nationalists, while pro-China coverage is more readily accepted among respondents with stronger prior regime support and nationalism.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Prior Foreign-Media Orientation]
Respondents with greater prior familiarity with, or trust in, foreign media are more likely to accept valenced messages, whereas low-trust respondents are more likely to discount anti-China coverage and reduce later demand.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

The next implication concerns repetition. The model predicts that dose works through cumulative accepted signals, not assignment count alone; repeated anti-China exposure among dissonant respondents should accumulate rejection rather than persuasion.
\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Dose]
Higher dose magnifies effects through acceptance: among receptive respondents, repetition strengthens persuasion, while among dissonant respondents repeated anti-China exposure intensifies rejection, backlash, and avoidance.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

\begin{singlespace}
\begin{hypothesis}[Persistence]
Effects persist when exposure changes both political beliefs and source credibility; pro-China effects are therefore more durable than anti-China effects, which decay more quickly when attended but not internalized.
\end{hypothesis}
\end{singlespace}

Taken together, these dynamics imply polarization: repeated valenced exposure should widen gaps between more nationalist and more regime-skeptical respondents when identical signals are converted into different acceptance decisions.

	\section*{Research Design}
	
	\subsection*{China's Information Environment and Experimental Platform}
	
China's media environment combines two features that matter for identification: tight control over routine news supply and incomplete closure of cross-border information flows. The party-state shapes domestic information through licensing, platform governance, and censorship enforcement, while the Great Firewall censors or bans many major foreign news outlets and platforms \citep{FreedomHouse2024,kingpanroberts2013,roberts2018,stockmann2013}. At the same time, citizens still encounter foreign content through VPN use, screenshot recirculation, reposting, and educational channels \citep{ChangEtAl2022,hobbsroberts2018,weiss2014powerful}. This coexistence is precisely the environment the theory targets: exposure is possible, but source cues remain politically charged.

The partner app provides a consequential setting for that question. Users are preparing for TOEFL, IELTS, GRE, GMAT, and related exams, so English-language reading is a normal part of study behavior rather than an unusual research task. In the baseline frame, recruited users are also measurably more foreign-oriented than non-recruited baseline users (higher foreign-news exposure and trust in foreign media, plus stronger study-abroad intent), which makes this a high-exposure population rather than a representative national sample.

All assignment, content delivery, and behavioral logging occurred inside the app backend. Each item appeared as a routine English-learning module: reading passage, short instructional video, quiz, and post-item evaluations. Embedding the intervention in this workflow keeps interface and participation burden constant while varying only the political valence and cumulative dose of the assigned material.
	
	\subsection*{Sample, Recruitment, and Assignment}
	
The baseline frame contains 12,215 users who completed the pre-treatment survey. Recruitment into the experimental cohort was selective by design, not a second random draw: the app prioritized users who passed quality checks and reported stronger study-abroad intent, then added substantial random noise within that eligible pool.\footnote{I do not use non-recruited baseline users as controls. They are used to characterize selection into the experimental population and for transport-style scope checks; causal estimates come from random assignment within the recruited sample.} This process produced 6,365 recruited participants (52.1\% of the baseline frame) who entered the 12-week intervention. Of these, 5,215 completed endline and 2,115 completed the four-week follow-up.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{figures/fig_flowchart.pdf}
	\caption{Study Flow and Treatment Assignment}
	\label{fig:study_flow}
\end{figure}
	
Randomization was individual-level within 24 strata defined by gender, macro-region, English proficiency, and baseline nationalism. Assignment was implemented by backend script, hidden from users and instructional staff, and yielded near-equal arm sizes (1,060--1,061 per arm). Realized baseline balance is strong across core covariates, and differential attrition by arm is statistically indistinguishable from zero at both post-treatment waves (endline joint \(p=.253\); follow-up joint \(p=.660\)).
	
	\subsection*{Treatment Arms and Delivery}
	
Each participant received 24 content slots over 12 weeks (two per week). Slot 1 carried the arm-specific treatment; slot 2 was always a neutral non-China filler. This design keeps assignment volume and weekly workload constant, so treatment effects are identified from the direction and dose of political content rather than from differential workload or platform exposure.

The six arms are Pro-low, Pro-high, Anti-low, Anti-high, Apolitical-China, and Non-China control. In high-dose arms, slot 1 contained directional China-related political content in all 12 weeks. In low-dose arms, slot 1 contained directional political content in six participant-specific randomized weeks and neutral filler in the other six weeks. The apolitical arm received non-directional China content in slot 1 each week; the control arm received neutral non-China content in both slots. This structure cleanly identifies two design contrasts: valence and cumulative political dose (12 versus 6 political assignments).

The final article bank contains 89 readings (24 Pro, 24 Anti, 17 Apolitical-China, 24 Non-China control). Materials were converted into standardized English-learning modules and assigned without within-person repetition. Content diagnostics show clear valence separation between Pro and Anti pools and near-zero centering for the two benchmark pools (Figure~\ref{fig:content_validity}).
	
	\begin{figure}[H]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_content_validity_tone.pdf}
		\caption{Content Validity of the Final Article Pools}
		\label{fig:content_validity}
	\end{figure}
	
After harmonization, non-substantive text characteristics are substantially closer across assigned bundles. Average sentence length and readability are similar across banks, while residual differences in article length are explicitly reported as design diagnostics rather than left implicit. Participation incentives were tied to completion and language-task performance (course bundle plus small quiz vouchers), never to political responses or treatment direction.
	
	\subsection*{Measurement and Outcomes}
	
The design measures three linked margins: attention, acceptance, and downstream demand. First, the app records slot-level behavioral data: openings, reading minutes, video completion, quiz outcomes, and weekly interest ratings. These outcomes identify whether politically valenced foreign content is consumed and whether it draws attention even when attitudes do not shift.
	
Second, the study measures acceptance through both contemporaneous evaluations and post-treatment attitudes. Weekly credibility ratings and ``want more similar'' ratings provide repeated indicators of whether assigned content is treated as believable and worth revisiting. Baseline, endline, and follow-up surveys measure regime support, censorship support, nationalism, the China--West thermometer gap, trust in foreign media, and content knowledge. Because these outcomes are observed before treatment, immediately after treatment, and four weeks later, the design distinguishes short-run updating from persistence and decay.
	
Third, the study measures downstream demand with consequential behavioral choices. At endline, participants completed an incentive-compatible WTP elicitation for additional foreign-news content using a Becker--DeGroot--Marschak (BDM) mechanism. The app recorded the bid, the randomized price draw, and the purchase decision. At follow-up, participants could opt into a later foreign-news digest, providing a zero-price revealed-preference measure of continued demand. Together these outcomes allow the paper to separate reading from believing and persuasion from later avoidance.
	
For parsimony, the main text emphasizes three standardized summary indices. The legitimacy index combines regime support, censorship support, and trust in domestic media sources. The foreign-media receptivity-and-demand index combines trust in foreign media, mean weekly credibility, mean weekly desire for similar content, willingness to pay, and the purchase indicator. The identity-backlash index combines nationalism, defensive-nationalism items, and the reverse of trust in foreign media. These indices map directly to the theory while preserving disaggregated item-level analyses in the appendix.
	
	\subsection*{Empirical Strategy and Inference}
	
The primary estimand is the intent-to-treat effect of assignment. Because exposure, engagement, and acceptance are themselves outcomes, the main specifications do not condition on post-treatment behavior. Endline and follow-up analyses use analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) specifications of the form:
	\[
	Y_{i1} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Pro}_i + \beta_2 \text{Anti}_i + \beta_3 \text{Apol}_i + \gamma Y_{i0} + \delta_b + \varepsilon_i,
	\]
where the omitted category is the non-China control arm, \(Y_{i0}\) is the baseline value of the outcome, and \(\delta_b\) are block fixed effects. The main presentation pools low and high dose within valence to isolate message direction; arm-level contrasts and signed-dose specifications then recover dose intensification. Continuous outcomes are estimated by ordinary least squares (OLS) and binary outcomes by linear probability models for comparability. Standard errors are heteroskedasticity-consistent type 2 (HC2), and purchase models additionally condition on the randomized BDM price draw.
	
Persistence is estimated in two complementary ways. First, follow-up ANCOVA models recover four-week post-treatment effects on the same outcomes. Second, individual fixed-effects panel models stack baseline, endline, and follow-up observations and interact treatment with survey wave, allowing direct tests of attenuation between endline and follow-up. Weekly outcomes are estimated on the slot-1 panel with week fixed effects, block fixed effects, and participant-clustered standard errors. This aligns weekly estimation with the treated margin of the design rather than pooling in the universal filler slot.
	
Inference is treated as part of design, not a post hoc add-on. The paper reports randomization-inference \(p\)-values based on within-block permutations that preserve realized treatment counts, Holm adjustments for the primary summary-index family, and attrition-robust checks using inverse-probability weighting and Lee bounds. For weekly outcomes, I also show robustness to zero-imputation after dropout and to inverse-probability weighting of observed weekly cells. Because the randomized sample is drawn from a larger baseline frame, I report selection diagnostics and transport-style checks; identification, however, comes entirely from random assignment within the recruited sample.
	
	\subsection*{Ethics, Preregistration, and Scope}
	
The study was preregistered before outcome analysis and reviewed by the University of California, San Diego (UCSD) Institutional Review Board. The paper treats the core valence and dose contrasts as preregistered tests. The implemented six-arm design preserves the registered comparisons between favorable, unfavorable, and neutral China-related content and between lower and higher cumulative political exposure, while simplifying some planned cells from earlier design documents. All participants were adult users and provided in-app consent before enrollment. The intervention was framed as an English-learning program, participation was voluntary, and incentives depended on completion and quiz performance rather than on political responses.
	
The main ethical risks were confidentiality and political sensitivity rather than public exposure. To reduce those risks, assignment, content delivery, and behavioral logging all occurred inside the partner app; the research team received only de-identified data keyed to anonymous study IDs; and personally identifying information remained on the firm's servers rather than in the analytic files. The materials were delivered as private educational exercises rather than as prompts to browse censored websites directly. These safeguards are especially important in the Chinese setting, where the project studies politically meaningful content inside a censored information environment.
	
This design has strong internal validity but a deliberately narrow target population. The estimand is not the average effect of censored foreign media on all Chinese citizens. It is the effect of repeated exposure among a population that is unusually likely to encounter English-language foreign content in everyday life. That scope condition is substantively central rather than incidental: if even this high-exposure population does not respond to censored foreign media in a uniform, liberalizing way, then simple access-based accounts of authoritarian information control are incomplete.
	
	\section*{Findings}
	
	\subsection*{Main Effects on Legitimacy, Receptivity, and Backlash}
	
	The cleanest main results are the summary indices reported in Table~\ref{tab:summary_indices}. Pro-China foreign coverage increases both legitimacy and subsequent receptivity to foreign media, while anti-China coverage reduces legitimacy and increases identity backlash. Specifically, pooled pro-China treatment raises the legitimacy index at endline by \(0.075\) standard deviations and the foreign-media receptivity and demand index by \(0.223\) standard deviations. Pooled anti-China treatment reduces the legitimacy index by \(0.033\) standard deviations and increases the backlash index by \(0.062\) standard deviations. These results survive stricter inference based on randomization inference and Holm correction.
	
	\input{tables/tab_summary_indices.tex}
	
	The arm-level outcome table shows the same directional pattern in the component outcomes. Relative to the non-China control arm, pro-China content substantially increases regime support, and the high-dose pro-China arm produces the strongest positive effect. Anti-China content lowers regime support in both dose conditions, with the high-dose anti-China arm generating the largest negative effect. At the same time, anti-China exposure increases nationalism rather than uniformly pushing respondents toward pro-Western views. This mixed response is the central substantive finding of the paper: foreign criticism can reduce regime support without generating straightforward liberalization.
	
	\input{tables/tab_main_ancova_t1.tex}
	
	\subsection*{Persistence Is Asymmetric}
	
	The follow-up results show that pro-China and anti-China messages do not decay in the same way. Table~\ref{tab:persistence_panel_fe} reports fixed-effects panel estimates that compare endline and follow-up treatment effects within respondents. Pooled pro-China treatment increases regime support by \(0.270\) at endline and still has a positive follow-up effect of \(0.110\), with attenuation but not disappearance. By contrast, pooled anti-China treatment reduces regime support by \(0.203\) at endline but has essentially no follow-up effect. The endline-to-follow-up change is statistically significant, indicating sharper decay for anti-China treatment.
	
	\input{tables/tab_persistence_panel_fe.tex}
	
	This asymmetry matters theoretically. If foreign media merely operated through temporary attention shocks, one might expect both pro- and anti-China effects to wash out at similar rates. Instead, positive foreign validation of China appears to generate more durable legitimating effects than negative foreign criticism generates durable delegitimating effects.
	
	Attrition sensitivity strengthens this conclusion rather than undermining it. Lee bounds, inverse-probability-weighted ANCOVA, and balanced-panel fixed-effects models all indicate that the follow-up pro-China legitimacy effect remains positive, while the anti-China follow-up legitimacy effect remains close to zero.
	
	\subsection*{Mechanisms: Attention, Acceptance, and Demand}
	
	The weekly and post-treatment behavioral data show that persuasion is not reducible to exposure alone. Table~\ref{tab:mechanism_chain} reports a mechanism chain spanning weekly engagement, weekly acceptance, and downstream demand. Both pro-China and anti-China political content increase total reading time by roughly 14 minutes over the study period relative to control. In other words, anti-China content does not simply cause avoidance at the attention stage.
	
	The divergence appears at acceptance and demand. Pro-China treatment increases weekly credibility ratings by \(2.900\) points and weekly demand for similar content by \(0.583\) points, while anti-China treatment produces neither effect. The downstream behavioral demand outcomes are even sharper: pro-China treatment raises willingness to pay by nearly 5 RMB and increases the purchase indicator by 11.7 percentage points, whereas anti-China treatment lowers willingness to pay by 2.8 RMB and lowers the purchase indicator by 5.1 percentage points.
	
	\input{tables/tab_mechanism_chain.tex}
	
	The weekly panel estimates reinforce the same interpretation. Pro-China content consistently increases credibility and interest. Anti-China content increases interest and reading time, but it reduces the desire to receive more similar content. Figure~\ref{fig:weekly_effects} shows that the pro-China credibility premium is visible across the weekly sequence, while anti-China content becomes especially negative on the ``want more similar'' dimension later in the intervention.
	
	\input{tables/tab_weekly_panel_slot1.tex}

	Together, these results favor a selective-acceptance interpretation rather than a selective-avoidance interpretation. Participants do read politically charged foreign content. The difference is that pro-China messages are more likely to be accepted as credible and converted into continued demand for foreign news, whereas anti-China messages attract attention without generating sustained appetite for more of the same source.
	
	\subsection*{Topic Heterogeneity}
	
	Treatment heterogeneity is not only about respondents. It is also about the type of foreign story participants see. Article-level topic diagnostics show that anti-China content is most negative when it focuses on repression, censorship, and broad social stress, while pro-China content is most positive when it emphasizes green development, technology, and macroeconomic performance. Weekly topic heterogeneity shows a parallel behavioral pattern: pro-China green-development and technology stories generate especially strong credibility and interest responses, while anti-China economy and property stories are more likely to depress willingness to consume more similar content.

	This heterogeneity clarifies why ``foreign media'' is too blunt a category. The political consequences of foreign reporting depend on whether foreign outlets criticize repression, criticize economic governance, praise policy performance, or cover China in nonpolitical cultural terms. Different topics produce different blends of attention, persuasion, and backlash.
	
	\subsection*{Scope Conditions}
	
	The internal validity of the design is strong, but the target population is narrow. The randomized sample consists of young Chinese users studying English for test preparation, many of whom are already more internationally oriented than the population at large. The paper should therefore not be read as estimating the average effect of foreign media on all Chinese citizens. Instead, it identifies the effects of repeated foreign-news exposure among a particularly relevant subpopulation: individuals who are plausibly among the most likely to encounter foreign-language reporting in everyday life.
	
	That scope condition does not weaken the core argument. If even this foreign-oriented population does not respond to anti-China foreign media in a uniformly liberalizing way, then simple access-based models of authoritarian persuasion are incomplete. The evidence instead points to a more conditional process in which valence, credibility, and repeated demand jointly determine political influence.
	
	\section*{Conclusion}
	
	Foreign media under authoritarianism do not produce a single political effect. In this study, Western reporting that framed China positively increased regime legitimacy and trust in foreign media, and some of those effects persisted after the intervention ended. Western reporting that framed China negatively reduced regime support in the short run, but it also generated identity backlash and did not translate into sustained demand for more foreign news. The contrast is clearest in the behavioral data: both kinds of political content hold attention, but only pro-China content is systematically converted into credibility and continued demand.
	
	The broader implication is that foreign source identity is not enough to predict persuasion. What matters is whether the foreign message validates or threatens the audience, whether it is repeated often enough to shape expectations, and whether recipients come to view the source as worth returning to. In authoritarian settings, then, foreign media can erode regime legitimacy, but they can also provide international legitimation. Understanding that dual possibility is essential for both theories of information control and debates about the political consequences of censorship circumvention.
	
	More generally, the study suggests that the politics of foreign information should be studied as a dynamic process that links exposure, credibility, and demand. Future work can extend this approach by testing how outlet labels, peer discussion, and algorithmic recommendation systems interact with message valence. But the core lesson of the present experiment is already clear: in authoritarian information environments, what foreign media say matters at least as much as the fact that they are foreign.

		\section{Formal Derivations and Proofs for the Minimal Model}
		\label{app:formal_proofs}
		
		This appendix derives the main comparative statics of the minimal model in the theory section. To keep notation light, I suppress respondent and time subscripts whenever no confusion will arise.
		
		A recipient is assigned one item per period. Before deciding whether to open it, the recipient observes a preview
		\[
		h=(c,\tilde v),
		\]
		where \(c\in\{0,1\}\) indicates whether the item concerns the focal polity and \(\tilde v\in\{-1,0,+1\}\) is a headline-level cue about whether the item appears unfavorable to it, non-directional, or favorable to it. After opening, the recipient observes full valence \(v\in\{-1,0,+1\}\). The recipient holds prior \(\mu\in(0,1)\) that the censored foreign outlet is independent rather than hostile.
		
		For focal-polity content, let
		\[
		\ell_j(v)=\Pr(v\mid s=j,c=1), \qquad j\in\{I,H\},
		\]
		denote the likelihood of observing valence \(v\) under an independent source \(I\) or a hostile source \(H\). Posterior source credibility after observing valence \(v\) is
		\[
		q(v;\mu)=\Pr(s=I\mid v,c=1)
		=\frac{\mu \ell_I(v)}{\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)}.
		\]
		
		The opening rule is
		\[
		r=\mathbf{1}\Big\{\alpha c+\sigma \mathbf{1}\{\tilde v\neq 0\}+\xi \bar q(h)-c^R\ge 0\Big\},
		\]
		where
		\[
		\bar q(h)\equiv \mathbb{E}[q(v;\mu)\mid h],
		\]
		\(\alpha>0\) captures topic salience, \(\sigma>0\) captures the additional pull of directional political content, \(\xi>0\) captures the value of potentially informative foreign material, and \(c^R\) is the private cost of opening the item.
		
		Conditional on exposure, the recipient accepts the message if
		\[
		a=\mathbf{1}\{U^A(v)\ge 0\},
		\]
		where
		\[
		U^A(v)=\lambda(v)q(v;\mu)-\rho^- p\,\mathbf{1}\{v=-1\}-\rho^+(1-p)\,\mathbf{1}\{v=+1\}-\tau n\,\mathbf{1}\{v=-1\}.
		\]
		Here \(p\in[0,1]\) indexes nationalism or prior regime support, with larger values indicating respondents more disposed to defend the regime; \(n\ge 0\) indexes susceptibility to foreign-threat interpretations; and all parameters are positive.
		
		The laws of motion are
		\[
		L_{t+1}=L_t+\beta_+ a_t\mathbf{1}\{v_t=+1\}-\beta_- a_t\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\},
		\]
		\[
		n_{t+1}=n_t+\eta r_t(1-a_t)\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\},
		\]
		\[
		\mu_{t+1}=(1-\omega)\mu_t+\omega q_t(v_t), \qquad \omega\in(0,1],
		\]
		and
		\[
		D_{t+1}=D_t+\phi a_t+\psi(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t)-\kappa r_t(1-a_t),
		\]
		with all parameters strictly positive.
		
		I now state and prove the propositions used in the main text.
		
		\begin{assumption}[Positive Support]
			\label{ass:positive_support}
			For each \(v\in\{-1,0,+1\}\), \(\ell_I(v)>0\) and \(\ell_H(v)>0\).
		\end{assumption}
		
		\begin{assumption}[Diagnostic Valence]
			\label{ass:diagnostic_valence}
			For focal-polity content,
			\[
			\frac{\ell_I(+1)}{\ell_H(+1)}>\frac{\ell_I(-1)}{\ell_H(-1)}.
			\]
			In addition, for the stronger comparative statics below,
			\[
			\ell_I(+1)>\ell_H(+1)
			\qquad\text{and}\qquad
			\ell_I(-1)<\ell_H(-1).
			\]
		\end{assumption}
		
		\begin{proposition}[Posterior ordering and diagnostic asymmetry]
			\label{prop:posterior_ordering}
			Under Assumptions \ref{ass:positive_support} and \ref{ass:diagnostic_valence},
			\[
			q(+1;\mu)>q(-1;\mu)
			\qquad\text{for all } \mu\in(0,1).
			\]
			Under the stronger sign restrictions in Assumption \ref{ass:diagnostic_valence},
			\[
			q(+1;\mu)>\mu>q(-1;\mu).
			\]
		\end{proposition}
		
		\begin{proof}
			Write posterior odds as
			\[
			\frac{q(v;\mu)}{1-q(v;\mu)}
			=
			\frac{\mu}{1-\mu}\cdot \frac{\ell_I(v)}{\ell_H(v)}.
			\]
			Because \(\mu/(1-\mu)>0\), the ordering of posterior odds across valences is determined by the likelihood ratio \(\ell_I(v)/\ell_H(v)\). Assumption \ref{ass:diagnostic_valence} implies
			\[
			\frac{q(+1;\mu)}{1-q(+1;\mu)}
			>
			\frac{q(-1;\mu)}{1-q(-1;\mu)}.
			\]
			Since the map \(x\mapsto x/(1+x)\) is strictly increasing on \((0,\infty)\), it follows that
			\[
			q(+1;\mu)>q(-1;\mu).
			\]
			
			For the second claim, compare each posterior to the prior. A direct calculation gives
			\[
			q(v;\mu)-\mu
			=
			\frac{\mu(1-\mu)\big(\ell_I(v)-\ell_H(v)\big)}
			{\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)}.
			\]
			The denominator is strictly positive by Assumption \ref{ass:positive_support}. Hence the sign of \(q(v;\mu)-\mu\) is the sign of \(\ell_I(v)-\ell_H(v)\). If \(\ell_I(+1)>\ell_H(+1)\), then \(q(+1;\mu)>\mu\). If \(\ell_I(-1)<\ell_H(-1)\), then \(q(-1;\mu)<\mu\). This proves the result.
		\end{proof}
		
		\begin{proposition}[Monotonicity of the opening rule]
			\label{prop:opening_monotonicity}
				The opening rule \(r\) is weakly increasing in (i) focal-polity relevance \(c\), (ii) the indicator for directional political content \(\mathbf{1}\{\tilde v\neq 0\}\), and (iii) expected posterior credibility \(\bar q(h)\).
		\end{proposition}
		
		\begin{proof}
			Define opening utility
			\[
			U^R(h)=\alpha c+\sigma \mathbf{1}\{\tilde v\neq 0\}+\xi \bar q(h)-c^R.
			\]
			Because \(\alpha,\sigma,\xi>0\), \(U^R(h)\) is weakly increasing in each of \(c\), \(\mathbf{1}\{\tilde v\neq 0\}\), and \(\bar q(h)\). The rule \(r=\mathbf{1}\{U^R(h)\ge 0\}\) is therefore weakly increasing in each argument as well.
		\end{proof}
		
		\begin{proposition}[Acceptance cutoffs]
			\label{prop:acceptance_cutoffs}
			Let \(\lambda_+=\lambda(+1)\), \(\lambda_-=\lambda(-1)\), and \(\lambda_0=\lambda(0)\).
			
			\begin{enumerate}
				\item For unfavorable content (\(v=-1\)),
				\[
				a=1
				\iff
				p\le p^-(\mu,n)
				\equiv
				\frac{\lambda_- q(-1;\mu)-\tau n}{\rho^-}.
				\]
				Hence acceptance is weakly increasing in \(\mu\) and weakly decreasing in \(p\) and \(n\).
				
				\item For favorable content (\(v=+1\)),
				\[
				a=1
				\iff
				p\ge p^+(\mu)
				\equiv
				1-\frac{\lambda_+ q(+1;\mu)}{\rho^+}.
				\]
				Hence acceptance is weakly increasing in \(\mu\) and weakly increasing in \(p\).
				
				\item For non-directional content (\(v=0\)), acceptance does not depend on \(p\) or \(n\), and is weakly increasing in \(\mu\).
			\end{enumerate}
		\end{proposition}
		
		\begin{proof}
			For \(v=-1\),
			\[
			U^A(-1)=\lambda_- q(-1;\mu)-\rho^- p-\tau n.
			\]
			Thus
			\[
			a=1
			\iff
			\lambda_- q(-1;\mu)-\rho^- p-\tau n\ge 0
			\iff
			p\le \frac{\lambda_- q(-1;\mu)-\tau n}{\rho^-}.
			\]
				This yields the unfavorable-content cutoff. Since \(\lambda_->0\) and \(q(-1;\mu)\) is increasing in \(\mu\) by Proposition \ref{prop:posterior_ordering} and the derivative established below in Proposition \ref{prop:trust_persistence}, the cutoff is increasing in \(\mu\). It is decreasing in \(n\) and, mechanically, acceptance is decreasing in \(p\).
			
			For \(v=+1\),
			\[
			U^A(+1)=\lambda_+ q(+1;\mu)-\rho^+(1-p).
			\]
			Thus
			\[
			a=1
			\iff
			\lambda_+ q(+1;\mu)-\rho^+(1-p)\ge 0
			\iff
			p\ge 1-\frac{\lambda_+ q(+1;\mu)}{\rho^+}.
			\]
				This yields the favorable-content cutoff. As above, since \(q(+1;\mu)\) is increasing in \(\mu\), the cutoff \(p^+(\mu)\) is decreasing in \(\mu\), so the set of \(p\) values for which acceptance occurs expands with \(\mu\). Acceptance is also increasing in \(p\).
			
			For \(v=0\),
			\[
			U^A(0)=\lambda_0 q(0;\mu),
			\]
			which depends on neither \(p\) nor \(n\), and is weakly increasing in \(\mu\).
		\end{proof}
		
		\begin{corollary}[Polarization through differential acceptance]
			\label{cor:polarization}
			Suppose two respondents \(A\) and \(B\) have the same \((\mu,n)\) but \(p_A>p_B\). If
			\[
			p_B<p^-(\mu,n)<p_A,
			\]
			then unfavorable content is accepted by \(B\) but rejected by \(A\). If
			\[
			p_B<p^+(\mu)<p_A,
			\]
			then favorable content is accepted by \(A\) but rejected by \(B\). In either case, exposure widens the gap in political attitudes between the two respondents.
		\end{corollary}
		
		\begin{proof}
				The first statement follows directly from the unfavorable-content cutoff in Proposition \ref{prop:acceptance_cutoffs}; the second follows from the favorable-content cutoff. If one respondent accepts a valenced signal and the other does not, the law of motion for legitimacy implies an immediate divergence in \(L\). Under unfavorable-message rejection, the law of motion for \(n\) also implies additional divergence through backlash. Hence exposure widens the attitudinal gap.
		\end{proof}
		
		\begin{proposition}[Rejected unfavorable exposure generates backlash and lowers demand]
			\label{prop:backlash_demand}
			Under the stronger sign restrictions in Assumption \ref{ass:diagnostic_valence}:
			
			\begin{enumerate}
				\item If \(r=1\), \(a=0\), and \(v=-1\), then
				\[
				n_{t+1}-n_t=\eta>0
				\]
				and
				\[
				D_{t+1}-D_t
				=
				\psi\big(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t\big)-\kappa
				=
				\psi\omega\big(q(-1;\mu_t)-\mu_t\big)-\kappa<0.
				\]
				
				\item If \(a=1\) and \(v=+1\), then
				\[
				\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t=\omega\big(q(+1;\mu_t)-\mu_t\big)>0
				\]
				and
				\[
				D_{t+1}-D_t
				=
				\phi+\psi\omega\big(q(+1;\mu_t)-\mu_t\big)>0.
				\]
			\end{enumerate}
		\end{proposition}
		
		\begin{proof}
			For part (1), if \(r=1\), \(a=0\), and \(v=-1\), the law of motion for \(n\) gives
			\[
			n_{t+1}-n_t=\eta r_t(1-a_t)\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\}=\eta>0.
			\]
			For demand,
			\[
			D_{t+1}-D_t
			=
			\phi a_t+\psi(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t)-\kappa r_t(1-a_t)
			=
			\psi(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t)-\kappa.
			\]
			Using the law of motion for \(\mu\),
			\[
			\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t
			=
			\omega\big(q(-1;\mu_t)-\mu_t\big).
			\]
			By Proposition \ref{prop:posterior_ordering}, \(q(-1;\mu_t)<\mu_t\), so the credibility term is negative. Since \(\kappa>0\), the whole expression is strictly negative.
			
			For part (2), if \(a=1\) and \(v=+1\), then
			\[
			\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t=\omega\big(q(+1;\mu_t)-\mu_t\big).
			\]
			By Proposition \ref{prop:posterior_ordering}, \(q(+1;\mu_t)>\mu_t\), so \(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t>0\). Demand changes by
			\[
			D_{t+1}-D_t
			=
			\phi+\psi(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t),
			\]
			which is strictly positive because \(\phi>0\) and \(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t>0\).
		\end{proof}
		
		\begin{proposition}[Trust accumulation and persistence]
			\label{prop:trust_persistence}
			For any fixed valence \(v\) with \(\ell_I(v),\ell_H(v)>0\), posterior credibility \(q(v;\mu)\) is strictly increasing in the prior \(\mu\). Specifically,
			\[
			\frac{\partial q(v;\mu)}{\partial \mu}
			=
			\frac{\ell_I(v)\ell_H(v)}
			{\big[\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)\big]^2}>0.
			\]
			Therefore, if two histories \(h\) and \(h'\) satisfy \(\mu_T(h)>\mu_T(h')\) and hold fixed \(p\), \(n_T\), and the future preview, then the history with higher \(\mu_T\) weakly increases future opening and acceptance.
		\end{proposition}
		
		\begin{proof}
			Differentiate
			\[
			q(v;\mu)=\frac{\mu \ell_I(v)}{\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)}
			\]
			with respect to \(\mu\). Using the quotient rule,
			\[
			\frac{\partial q(v;\mu)}{\partial \mu}
			=
			\frac{\ell_I(v)\big[\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)\big]-\mu \ell_I(v)\big[\ell_I(v)-\ell_H(v)\big]}
			{\big[\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)\big]^2}.
			\]
			The numerator simplifies to \(\ell_I(v)\ell_H(v)\), so
			\[
			\frac{\partial q(v;\mu)}{\partial \mu}
			=
			\frac{\ell_I(v)\ell_H(v)}
			{\big[\mu \ell_I(v)+(1-\mu)\ell_H(v)\big]^2}>0.
			\]
			
			Now compare two histories \(h\) and \(h'\) with \(\mu_T(h)>\mu_T(h')\). Since \(q(v;\mu)\) is increasing in \(\mu\), the higher-\(\mu\) history yields a weakly larger posterior for any future valence. The opening rule is increasing in expected posterior credibility by Proposition \ref{prop:opening_monotonicity}, and the acceptance rule is increasing in posterior credibility by Proposition \ref{prop:acceptance_cutoffs}. Hence higher accumulated source trust weakly increases future receptivity, holding fixed \(p\), \(n_T\), and the preview.
		\end{proof}
		
		\begin{proposition}[Dose decomposition]
			\label{prop:dose_decomposition}
			After \(T\) periods, the state variables admit the following decomposition:
			\[
			L_T
			=
			L_0
			+
			\beta_+ \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} a_t \mathbf{1}\{v_t=+1\}
			-
			\beta_- \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} a_t \mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\},
			\]
			\[
			n_T
			=
			n_0
			+
			\eta \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} r_t(1-a_t)\mathbf{1}\{v_t=-1\},
			\]
			\[
			\mu_T
			=
			(1-\omega)^T\mu_0
			+
			\omega \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(1-\omega)^{T-1-t} q_t(v_t),
			\]
			and
			\[
			D_T
			=
			D_0
			+
			\phi \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} a_t
			+
			\psi(\mu_T-\mu_0)
			-
			\kappa \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} r_t(1-a_t).
			\]
				Consequently, an additional dose affects legitimacy only through accepted signals, increases backlash only through rejected unfavorable signals, and affects downstream demand through the balance of accepted exposure, rejected exposure, and accumulated credibility.
		\end{proposition}
		
		\begin{proof}
			The expressions for \(L_T\) and \(n_T\) follow by iterating their one-period laws of motion and summing over \(t=0,\dots,T-1\).
			
			For \(\mu_T\), repeatedly substitute
			\[
			\mu_{t+1}=(1-\omega)\mu_t+\omega q_t(v_t).
			\]
			A standard forward recursion yields
			\[
			\mu_T
			=
			(1-\omega)^T\mu_0
			+
			\omega \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(1-\omega)^{T-1-t} q_t(v_t).
			\]
			
			For \(D_T\), sum the demand law of motion over \(t\):
			\[
			D_T-D_0
			=
			\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\phi a_t
			+
			\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\psi(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t)
			-
			\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\kappa r_t(1-a_t).
			\]
			The middle term telescopes:
			\[
			\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(\mu_{t+1}-\mu_t)=\mu_T-\mu_0.
			\]
			Substituting gives
			\[
			D_T
			=
			D_0
			+
			\phi \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} a_t
			+
			\psi(\mu_T-\mu_0)
			-
			\kappa \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} r_t(1-a_t).
			\]
			
				The final claim follows immediately from these decompositions. Legitimacy changes only through accepted positive and negative signals. Foreign-threat sensitivity changes only through rejected unfavorable exposure. Demand depends on the cumulative number of accepted items, the cumulative number of rejected items, and the net change in source credibility.
		\end{proof}
		
		Propositions \ref{prop:posterior_ordering}--\ref{prop:dose_decomposition} formalize the main claims in the text. Politically valenced censored foreign content should attract attention, but its downstream effects depend on whether recipients accept it as informative. Because positive coverage is more diagnostic of source independence, it is more likely to raise both source trust and future demand. Negative coverage can reduce regime legitimacy, but it is also more vulnerable to reinterpretation as hostile and therefore more likely to generate backlash, lower subsequent demand, and weaker persistence.
