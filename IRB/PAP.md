### Description

This research project, "Breaking the Great Fire Wall: A Field Experiment on Western-Media Exposure in China," aims to investigate the impact of exposure to uncensored Western media on political attitudes and English language skills among Chinese citizens.  Purpose of the Research: The core purpose of this study is to empirically evaluate whether information from abroad can penetrate an authoritarian media environment, specifically China's Great Firewall, and, if so, what effect it has on regime legitimacy and support. While classical theories predict that exposure to uncensored foreign news will erode support for authoritarian rule, empirical evidence has been mixed. This experiment seeks to address this gap by providing direct, exogenous exposure to Western media, thereby offering a clearer understanding of the causal relationship between information access and political attitudes in an authoritarian context. Additionally, the study explores a potential "co-benefit" of circumventing censorship: improved English language proficiency, given that much of the blocked content is in English.  Research Design: The study employs a field experiment involving 1,000 Chinese university students. Participants will be randomly assigned to either a treatment group (N=500) or a control group (N=500). The treatment group will receive a free, premium virtual private network (VPN) service for two months, along with weekly push notifications encouraging them to use the VPN to access Western news sources (e.g., BBC, NYT, CNN, foreign-language YouTube news channels). The control group will not receive the VPN or the push notifications.  Key Hypotheses: The study will test several key hypotheses, broadly categorized into: 1. VPN Usage: The treatment group will exhibit significantly higher VPN usage rates than the control group. 2. Western Media Exposure: The treatment group will report significantly higher consumption of Western news media compared to the control group. 3. Political Attitudes (Regime Support): Exposure to Western media will lead to a decrease in the treatment group's support for the Chinese regime. This will be measured by various indicators, including pride in Chinese citizenship, perceived superiority of China's political system, trust in the government, and agreement with statements related to nationalistic narratives. 4. English Proficiency: Exposure to Western media will lead to an improvement in the treatment group's English language skills, as measured by a standardized English proficiency test.  Outcome Measures: Data will be collected through a baseline survey administered before the experiment and a follow-up survey after the two-month intervention period. Key outcome measures include: • Media Consumption Habits: Frequency of consuming news from various Chinese (state-run, commercial, social media) and foreign sources (English news websites, foreign-language YouTube news channels). • Trust in Media Platforms: Self-reported trust levels for various media platforms. • Political Attitudes: A multi-item "Regime Support Index" assessing agreement with statements related to national pride, political system superiority, trust in government, and views on Western criticism. Feeling thermometers for China and the US will also be used to gauge affective attitudes. • English Language Proficiency: A standardized English proficiency test (e.g., Duolingo English Test or equivalent) administered at both baseline and follow-up. • VPN Usage Data: Usage logs from the VPN provider (where available and consented to) will serve as a proxy for actual exposure to Western media. Expected Outcomes: The expected outcomes include: • Empirical Evidence on Censorship Evasion: Demonstrating the effectiveness of VPNs in circumventing censorship and facilitating access to otherwise blocked information. • Causal Impact on Political Attitudes: Providing robust causal evidence on how exposure to diverse information sources, particularly Western media, influences citizens' political attitudes and support for an authoritarian government. This could offer insights into the resilience or fragility of information control. • Understanding "Co-benefits": Quantifying the potential indirect benefits, such as improved English language skills, that may arise from engaging with foreign media. • Informing Policy and Theory: The findings will contribute to the theoretical understanding of authoritarian information control and media effects, and may inform policy discussions regarding digital freedoms and information access globally. • Generalizability: While focused on China, the findings may offer insights into the dynamics of information control and citizen responses in other authoritarian or semi-authoritarian contexts. This research aims to significantly advance our understanding of how individuals navigate and are affected by information environments shaped by state censorship.

#### Study design

 Field Experiment: Conducted in a real-world setting among Chinese English examine taker students to enhance ecological validity. Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT): Participants are randomly assigned to different experimental conditions, ensuring that, on average, unobserved characteristics are balanced across groups to establish causality. Randomized Block Design: Prior to assignment, participants are "Block-randomized by demographics and ideology." This ensures that key participant characteristics are evenly distributed across all experimental groups, increasing statistical power and reducing confounding. Multi-group Design: There are a total of six distinct experimental arms, including the factorial treatment groups and two separate control groups. Experimental Arms (Between-Subjects):   The  participants are block-randomized into the following six distinct groups:   1. Factorial Treatment Groups (2x2 Factorial Design): These groups receive exposure to specific types of content (Pro-China or Anti-China) at varying dosages.    Content Valence Factor:      Pro-China: Participants receive news content with a pro-China sentiment.      Anti-China: Participants receive news content with an anti-China (or critical of China) sentiment.    Dosage Factor:      Low Dose (5 pieces): Participants receive 5 pieces of news content per week.      High Dose (20 pieces): Participants receive 20 pieces of news content per week.    This creates four unique treatment conditions:      Pro-China Low Dose      Pro-China High Dose      Anti-China Low Dose      Anti-China High Dose  2. Pure Control Groups: These groups serve as additional baselines for comparison beyond just the simple absence of a specific treatment.    Control: This is a standard control group, likely receiving no specific news content intervention or VPN access (consistent with the PDF's general control group concept).    Apolitical: This group likely receives apolitical content (e.g., general interest news, lifestyle, sports) or no specific news content, to isolate the effect of political content exposure. Measurement Timing (Repeated Measures / Within-Subjects):  Baseline Survey: Administered to all, before the intervention. Weekly and Endline Survey: Administered to participants. This indicates regular, repeated measurements throughout and at the conclusion of the main intervention period. The reduction in N suggests some attrition. Follow-up Survey: Administered to participants. This provides a longer-term assessment of effects, acknowledging further attrition.

#### Randomization

In this randomized study, the randomization will be performed using a block randomization technique.  Method of Randomization: Participants will be block-randomized by demographics and ideology. This method involves dividing the participant pool into smaller groups (blocks) based on specific demographic and ideological characteristics (e.g., gender, age group, baseline political leaning). Within each block, participants are then randomly assigned to the different experimental conditions. This ensures that the key covariates (demographics and ideology) are balanced across all the experimental arms at the outset of the study, thereby increasing the statistical power and reducing potential confounding. Level of Randomization: Randomization will occur at the individual participant level. Each eligible participant (from the pool after prescreening and consent) will be assigned to one of the six experimental arms (Pro-China Low Dose, Pro-China High Dose, Anti-China Low Dose, Anti-China High Dose, Control, Apolitical).

#### Data collection procedures Updated

Participants will be Chinese citizens aged 18 to 25 preparing for standardized English exams such as TOEFL, IELTS, GRE, or GMAT. Recruitment will occur through in-app invitations sent to users of a major Chinese test preparation platform with over 260,000 registered users. The invitation will describe a voluntary “Authentic English News Study Camp” framed as an English-learning activity. Inclusion criteria are Chinese citizenship, age eighteen or older, intermediate or higher English proficiency based on test scores or in-app level, and willingness to spend about two to three hours per week for twelve weeks. Exclusion criteria are incomplete pre-screening survey, failed comprehension or attention checks, and duplicate or bot accounts identified through backend data. Eligible participants who consent will be stratified into twenty-four blocks based on gender, region, English proficiency, and baseline nationalism. Within each block, participants will be randomly assigned by an automated app script to one of eight experimental conditions. Randomization will be invisible to both users and instructors. The intervention lasts twelve weeks. Each Monday, participants receive two English-language articles and corresponding instructional videos drawn from The New York Times, The Economist, The Wall Street Journal, or The Washington Post. Assignments depend on treatment condition: pro-China, anti-China, mixed, apolitical, or grammar-only control. Participants complete comprehension quizzes and feedback forms each week, and all reading, viewing, and quiz data are automatically logged by the app. Students who complete all modules receive a premium course bundle worth about 500 RMB and can earn weekly quiz bonuses of 5 to 10 RMB. Payments are automated through the in-app wallet and are based only on participation and quiz completion, not on political responses. All behavioral and survey data remain on the app provider’s encrypted servers. The research team receives only de-identified data. Personally identifiable information is never shared and remains encrypted at rest. The overall timeline is two weeks for recruitment and screening, twelve weeks for the experimental intervention, one week for the endline survey, and four weeks later for the follow-up survey.

#### Sample size Updated

The planned sample includes approximately 3,000 individual participants, each serving as a single unit of analysis. Participants will be university-age Chinese students enrolled in standardized English test-preparation programs. This study is a fully individual-level randomized controlled trial with no clustering in assignment or analysis. Randomization will occur within twenty-four blocks formed by stratifying on gender, English proficiency, region, and baseline nationalism. Each participant will be randomly assigned to one of eight experimental arms, consisting of three framing types by two dosage levels, plus an apolitical condition and a pure control group. Clustering or nesting effects are expected to be minimal since all content is delivered individually through a mobile app, and all engagement data are recorded at the user level. Analyses will focus on individual outcomes such as political attitudes, media trust, and behavioral indicators including article ratings and willingness to pay for foreign news. Power simulations show that with a total sample of 3,000 participants, the study achieves 80 percent statistical power to detect standardized effects of approximately 0.28, even allowing for up to 15 percent attrition.

#### Sample size rationale Updated

The target sample of 3,000 participants is determined by both statistical power considerations and practical constraints. With individual-level randomization, two-tailed tests at alpha = 0.05, and 80% power, a sample of 3,000 (approximately 375 participants per arm across eight groups) allows detection of standardized effects of about 0.28 SD on primary outcomes, assuming equal allocation and up to 15% attrition. Incorporating baseline covariates and blocking variables (gender, region, English proficiency, nationalism) is expected to improve precision, effectively lowering the minimum detectable effect to around 0.25 SD. Operationally, this sample size reflects the capacity of the partner platform to recruit within a two-week window and fits within the available budget for incentives and research personnel. Participation bonuses (approximately 500 RMB per completer plus weekly quiz rewards) make larger samples financially impractical without reducing power below acceptable thresholds. The final target of 3,000 balances sufficient statistical power with time, funding, and logistical feasibility for a 12-week in-app intervention.

#### Measured variables Updated

Primary Outcomes (measured at baseline t0, endline t1, and follow-up t2 unless noted) 1. Regime Support Index (t0, t1, t2): mean of five items (economic development, social stability, social equality, technological progress, government efficiency). Each item on 1–5 Likert (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree) to statements of satisfaction. Index standardized to mean 0, SD 1 at t0; higher scores = greater regime support. 2. Censorship Attitudes Scale (t0, t1, t2): mean of three items on 1–5 Likert endorsing content restriction (e.g., “Restricting certain online content is necessary for social stability”). Higher = stronger support for censorship. 3. China–West Feeling Thermometer Gap (t0, t1, t2): 0–100 rating for China minus 0–100 rating for “Western democracies.” Negative values = relatively warmer feelings toward West; positive = warmer toward China. 4. Nationalism Battery (t0, t1, t2): mean of four 1–5 Likert items reflecting patriotic attachment and defensive nationalism (e.g., “Foreign criticism of China is usually biased”). Higher = stronger nationalism. 5. Willingness to Pay for Foreign News (t1; incentive-compatible): Becker–DeGroot–Marschak auction. Participant states maximum 0–50 RMB to receive a one-month bundle from a chosen outlet (NYT, Economist, WSJ, WP). App draws uniform random price 0–50. Outcome variables: stated WTP (RMB), purchase indicator (1 if drawn price ≤ WTP), chosen outlet. Secondary Outcomes 6) Media Trust—Domestic State (t0, t1, t2): single 1–5 item assessing trust in state-run outlets (higher = more trust). 7) Media Trust—Domestic Market (t0, t1, t2): single 1–5 item for commercial Chinese media. 8) Media Trust—Western Media (t0, t1, t2): single 1–5 item for Western/foreign outlets. 9) Perceived Media Bias (t2): 0–10 ratings where 0 = completely objective, 10 = extremely biased for (a) Chinese domestic media, (b) Western media reporting on China. 10) Knowledge Index (t1): proportion correct on 10 multiple-choice factual questions drawn from assigned content, plus two placebo items for discrimination; index is (# correct)/12 and an alternative score using only the 10 content items. Behavioral and Process Measures (from app logs, continuous over study) 11) Article Reception Indicators: per-week binary R_it = 1 if article opened and paired video reached ≥70% watch time; summed across 12 weeks. 12) Video Engagement: total minutes watched and proportion of each video viewed (0–1) per article; averaged and totaled per participant. 13) Reading Duration: total minutes reading per article (app-tracked), winsorized at 99th percentile; summed and averaged per participant. 14) Quiz Accuracy: per-article 10-item quiz score (0–10); weekly and overall averages. 15) Article Liking (Mediator—Acceptance Proxy): 0–100 slider after each article; participant-level mean and within-person SD. 16) Article Credibility (Mediator—Acceptance Proxy): 0–100 slider after each article; participant-level mean and within-person SD. 17) “Read More Similar” Intention: categorical (Yes/Maybe/No) per article; recode to 1/0 for Yes vs non-Yes and separate ordinal 2/1/0. 18) Completion/Compliance Flags: weekly compliant = watch-time threshold met and quiz score ≥8/10; proportion of compliant weeks (0–1). 19) Follow-up Signup (t2): indicator if participant opts into free one-week foreign-news digest; 1/0. Treatment and Manipulation Variables 20) Assigned Frame: categorical with levels Pro-China, Anti-China, Mixed, Apolitical, Grammar-Only Control. Dummy variables for analysis. 21) Assigned Dosage: categorical Low (5 political articles over study) vs High (20 political articles). For Mixed, dose refers to political content count; apolitical receives 24 neutral; control receives 24 textbook passages. 22) Delivered Dose: count of political articles actually unlocked to participant (exposure opportunity). 23) Received Dose: count of political articles with R_it = 1 (opened + video threshold met). 24) Topic Track: topic labels (Economics, Domestic Development, Culture & Society, Science & Technology) for each article; participant-level counts. Moderators and Predictors (measured at t0 unless stated) 25) Baseline Ideology/Regime Support (t0): the t0 Regime Support Index (standardized) used both as covariate and moderator. 26) Baseline Nationalism (t0): the t0 Nationalism Battery (standardized) as moderator. 27) Prior Foreign Exposure (t0): frequency of foreign media use in past month (5-point scale: never to very often) and platform accounts used (YouTube, Instagram, etc., multiple selection). Construct index by z-scoring frequency plus platform count. 28) VPN Proxy (t0): indicator if accessed foreign platforms (e.g., YouTube, Twitter, Instagram, foreign news sites) in past 90 days; 1 = any, 0 = none. 29) Media Habits (t0): six 1–5 frequency items (state-run, commercial Chinese, social media, foreign news sites, foreign-language YouTube, news via chat). Store as separate variables and a standardized composite for “Foreign Media Frequency.” 30) English Proficiency (t0): latest standardized score (TOEFL, IELTS band, GRE Verbal, GMAT Verbal); harmonized z-score and categorical (above/below median) for blocking. 31) Demographics (t0): gender (male/female), age (years), ethnicity (Han/minority), region (East/Central/West), city (string), education level (associate/bachelor/master/PhD). 32) Study Abroad Plans (t0): 4-point intention scale (definitely yes to definitely not) and binary indicators. 33) Travel Abroad Experience (t0): yes/no indicator. Additional Attitudinal Measures (t0, t1, t2) 34) Censorship Items (component-level): store item scores separately for item-level analyses and scale reliability. 35) Policy Beliefs by Theme (t1): four single-item policy statements aligned to topic clusters; 1–5 Likert; also difference-from-baseline if asked at t0. Data Quality and Diagnostics 36) Attention Checks: two per survey wave; binary pass/fail per wave; global count 0–2 per wave and 0–6 across waves. 37) Response Time Flags: per survey page below 5th percentile flagged; per-respondent count; used for robustness exclusions. 38) Straightlining Index: within-battery variance; low variance flags potential satisficing; binary flag and continuous measure. 39) Duplicate/Bot Flag: backend heuristic (device ID duplication, abnormal timing, impossible throughput); binary exclusion variable. 40) Attrition Indicators: endline completion (t1) 1/0; follow-up completion (t2) 1/0. Constructed Change Scores (analysis-ready) 41) Δ Regime Support = t1 − t0; also t2 − t0. 42) Δ Censorship Support = t1 − t0; also t2 − t0. 43) Δ China–West Gap = t1 − t0; also t2 − t0. 44) Δ Nationalism = t1 − t0; also t2 − t0. 45) Dose-Response Exposure: continuous total of received political articles and total political minutes watched. 46) Mediation Composite—Acceptance: participant-level mean of standardized liking and credibility across political articles. Coding and Scaling Notes • All multi-item indices are averaged then standardized at t0 (mean 0, SD 1) for interpretability; higher values reflect more of the named construct. • Likert items are coded so higher numbers correspond to stronger endorsement of the construct (reverse-code where needed, e.g., “People should be allowed to access all information online freely”). • For mixed models, treatment dummies will be compared to apolitical and pure control groups; sensitivity checks pool apolitical and control. • Outlier handling: cap reading and watch-time at 99th percentile; retain uncapped values for robustness. • Missingness: item nonresponse indicators are recorded; multiple imputation models will condition on baseline covariates and treatment. #### Statistical models Updated

General notes • Unit: individual participant. • Estimation: OLS with HC2/HC3 robust SEs and block fixed effects unless stated. No clustering (assignment is individual). • Blocks: 24 strata (gender × region × English proficiency × baseline nationalism). • Covariates (X): baseline value of the outcome (ANCOVA), plus gender, region, English proficiency (z), baseline nationalism (z). • Treatment coding: Frame ∈ {Anti, Pro, Mixed, Apolitical, Control}. Dosage ∈ {Low, High} for political frames; Apolitical receives neutral articles; Control receives grammar-only passages. • Primary contrasts: Anti vs Apolitical; Pro vs Apolitical; Mixed vs Apolitical. Key secondary: Anti vs Pro. • Multiple testing: Holm–Bonferroni across the 3 primary frame contrasts for each primary outcome; Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR 5%) within families of secondary outcomes. • Missingness/attrition: ITT as primary; IPW and Lee bounds as sensitivity. Variables used below • Y: outcome at endline t1 (or follow-up t2). • Y0: baseline outcome (t0). • T_Anti, T_Pro, T_Mixed, T_Apol (binary dummies; Control is omitted). • Dose_Hi (1 = high political dose, 0 = low). #### Exploratory analysis Updated

Exploratory analyses will be conducted to identify additional patterns and mechanisms beyond the pre-registered hypotheses. These analyses will be clearly described as exploratory if reported. 1. Subgroup exploration will test whether treatment effects vary by gender, region, English proficiency, or study abroad intentions through interaction models. 2. Latent class or cluster analysis will be used to identify unobserved ideological profiles based on baseline nationalism, regime support, and media trust, and treatment effects will be re-estimated within these groups. 3. Article level engagement patterns will be explored to see if topic, length, or sentiment predict reading time, quiz accuracy, or liking independent of treatment condition using mixed effects models. 4. Time dynamics will be examined by tracking changes in engagement and attitudes week by week during the twelve week intervention using fixed effects or growth curve models. 5. Dose response nonlinearity will be assessed by estimating nonparametric curves such as splines or loess fits to detect diminishing returns or saturation at high exposure levels. 6.
