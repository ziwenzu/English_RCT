# External Validity Scope

## Ready-to-use framing

This study identifies the effects of repeated foreign-news exposure among a politically and educationally distinctive subgroup of young Chinese test-preparation users rather than among the general Chinese public. Relative to baseline respondents who were not recruited into the randomized study, recruited participants were substantially more likely to pass baseline quality checks, less likely to straightline, more interested in studying abroad, more exposed to foreign news, more trusting of foreign media, and less supportive of the regime, censorship, and nationalist narratives at baseline. The experimental estimates therefore have strong internal validity for a foreign-oriented, English-learning population with regular interest in overseas information, but they should not be interpreted as nationally representative treatment effects.

## Short paper version

The sample is not population-representative. Recruitment favored respondents who passed baseline quality checks and showed stronger foreign orientation. Compared with non-recruited baseline respondents, recruited participants were more likely to plan study abroad, consumed more foreign news, trusted foreign media more, and held less pro-regime and less nationalist baseline attitudes. I therefore interpret the experiment as identifying treatment effects among a substantively important but selective subgroup of young Chinese English learners who are unusually likely to encounter foreign information in practice.
